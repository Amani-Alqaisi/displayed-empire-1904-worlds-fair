# Checkpoint Draft

## 1) Title and brief overview of the final project

**Title:** Displayed Empire: The 1904 St. Louis World’s Fair, Human Exhibits, and the Politics of Race

**Brief overview:**  
Our final project is a GitHub Pages website that examines how the 1904 St. Louis World’s Fair used the Department of Anthropology, the Philippine Reservation, and related live exhibits to present racial hierarchy and American empire as normal, educational, and even entertaining. Rather than treating the fair as only a celebration of innovation, our website focuses on how people were put on display and classified in ways that supported racist and imperial ideas. The website uses short analysis sections, a timeline, and outside sources to show how science, spectacle, and public memory worked together at the fair.

## 2) What readings/resources from outside the course have been most relevant?

The most relevant outside sources have been the **Official Guide to the Louisiana Purchase Exposition** and the **Official Directory of the Louisiana Purchase Exposition**, because they show how the fair officially described itself and how the Philippine Reservation was structured. Richard Kennedy’s Smithsonian essay, **“Rethinking the Philippine Exhibit at the 1904 St. Louis World’s Fair,”** has also been especially helpful because it explains how the exhibit supported U.S. imperial goals in the Philippines. Another important source is Susan Brownell’s edited volume, **The 1904 Anthropology Days and Olympic Games: Sport, Race, and American Imperialism**, which helps connect the fair to public performances of racial hierarchy. Museum and public history sources from the **St. Louis Art Museum**, the **Missouri History Museum**, and **PBS NewsHour** have also been useful for understanding how this history is being interpreted now.
