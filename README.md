# Displayed Empire: The 1904 St. Louis World’s Fair, Human Exhibits, and the Politics of Race

**Created by:** Amani Alqaisi and Anett Martinez Vazquez  
**Live website:** https://amani-alqaisi.github.io/displayed-empire-1904-worlds-fair/

## Project Overview

This project is a GitHub Pages website about the 1904 St. Louis World’s Fair, with a focus on the Department of Anthropology, the Philippine Reservation, Anthropology Days, Ota Benga, and the broader politics of racialized public display. Rather than presenting the fair only as a celebration of progress and innovation, the site studies how it turned empire, race, and hierarchy into something public, visual, and seemingly educational.

The website combines historical research, image analysis, and course themes from signals, data, and equity. It argues that the fair used architecture, labels, photographs, performances, and measurement to teach visitors how to interpret colonized and racialized people through a colonial lens.

## Main Questions

- How did the 1904 St. Louis World’s Fair present race and empire to the public?
- How were living people displayed as part of the fair’s educational and entertainment systems?
- How did anthropology and visual spectacle work together to make racial hierarchy seem normal?
- Why does this history still matter when thinking about signals, data, and equity?

## Topics Covered

- the Philippine Reservation
- Anthropology Days
- Ota Benga and the politics of bodily display

- public memory and reinterpretation
