# Displayed Empire: The 1904 St. Louis World’s Fair, Human Exhibits, and the Politics of Race

**Created by:** Amani Alqaisi and Anett Martinez Vazquez

**Live website:** https://amani-alqaisi.github.io/displayed-empire-1904-worlds-fair/

## Project Overview

This project is a GitHub Pages website that examines the 1904 St. Louis World’s Fair, with a focus on the use of human exhibits, the Department of Anthropology, the Philippine Reservation, Anthropology Days, and other cases of racialized display. Rather than presenting the fair only as a celebration of progress and innovation, this site studies how it turned empire, race, and hierarchy into something public, visual, and seemingly educational.

The website looks at how organizers used architecture, photography, anthropology, performance, and classification to shape the way visitors understood colonized and racialized people. It also considers how this history has been remembered, reinterpreted, and challenged in later scholarship and museum work.

## Main Questions

This project asks:

- How did the 1904 St. Louis World’s Fair present race and empire to the public?
- How were human beings displayed as part of the fair’s educational and entertainment systems?
- How did anthropology and visual spectacle work together to make racial hierarchy seem normal?
- Why does this history still matter when thinking about signals, data, and equity?

## Topics Covered

The website includes sections on:

- the historical context of the fair
- the Philippine Reservation
- Anthropology Days
- Ota Benga and other racialized exhibits
- visual analysis of historical images
- public memory and reinterpretation
- the connection between this history and the themes of signals, data, and equity

## Why This Project Matters

This project argues that the fair did more than display objects and new technology. It displayed people in ways that supported U.S. imperial power and racial hierarchy. By studying the fair through this lens, the site shows how systems of classification, spectacle, and institutional authority can make inequality appear natural or objective.

## Sources

The project draws on a mix of primary and secondary sources, including:

- *Official Guide to the Louisiana Purchase Exposition* (1904)
- *Official Directory of the Louisiana Purchase Exposition* (1904)
- Bureau of Insular Affairs materials on the Philippine exhibit
- Susan Brownell’s work on Anthropology Days
- Richard Kennedy’s writing on the Philippine exhibit
- Missouri History Museum and related public history resources
- Smithsonian and Washington University archival materials
- historical image collections from public-domain and museum-linked sources

A fuller annotated list is included in `sources.md`.
